# Editorial Strategy

## Positioning

The series should make psychological concepts feel like something readers have
already lived through: the unanswered Slack message, the over-explained apology,
the 11 p.m. cart review, the "I'm fine" that means three different things, the
meeting where one person wants certainty and another wants permission.

Lacey's brand should become:

- warm, precise, and human;
- academic enough to be trustworthy;
- practical enough to use the same day;
- funny without turning distress into a punchline;
- careful about boundaries: education and reflection, not diagnosis.

The soft-launch path for PsychSimulator is indirect. Early posts should build
trust in the concepts and in Lacey's voice. Later posts can introduce simulation,
synthetic scenarios, reproducibility, and "what if we could rehearse hard moments
before they happen?" without making product claims before validation is ready.

## Audience

Primary readers:

- professionals who recognize stress, conflict, burnout, people-pleasing, and
  decision fatigue in work life;
- adults trying to understand relationship patterns without clinical jargon;
- therapy-curious readers who want concepts they can try safely;
- community members who may later become pilot users, contributors, or advocates.

Secondary readers:

- therapists, coaches, researchers, and educators who care about provenance and
  responsible framing;
- AI builders interested in transparent psychological simulation.

## Editorial Promise

Each post should give readers three things:

1. A story they recognize.
2. A concept that names the pattern without shaming the person.
3. One small experiment they can try in real life.

## Voice Rules

- Start with a scene, not a definition.
- Use humor from recognition, not ridicule.
- Prefer "this pattern can happen when..." over "you are..."
- Say what a concept is useful for and what it cannot prove.
- Keep clinical terms grounded in everyday behavior.
- Avoid turning the simulator's engine labels into public personality labels.
- Cite or link sources when the post makes a research claim.

## Content Pillars

1. **Everyday therapy concepts**
   Schemas, needs, coping, appraisal, attachment, emotion regulation, decision
   styles, and context activation in normal life.

2. **Workplace patterns**
   Feedback, performance pressure, conflict, authority, decision fatigue,
   status, fairness, and team misreadings.

3. **Relationship patterns**
   Reassurance, withdrawal, over-functioning, repair attempts, boundaries,
   misattunement, and emotional bids.

4. **Self-reflection tools**
   Prompts, tiny experiments, conversation scripts, journaling questions, and
   "try this before the spiral gets a calendar invite" style practical moves.

5. **Responsible AI and simulation**
   Later-stage posts on synthetic profiles, provenance, reproducibility,
   evaluation, and what simulation can and cannot responsibly claim.

## Quality Bar

A publishable post should pass this checklist:

- The opening scene is specific enough to feel lived-in.
- The psychology concept is explained in plain language.
- Claims map back to a reviewed source, wiki concept, or simulator construct.
- The post avoids diagnosis, therapeutic advice beyond general education, and
  unsupported certainty.
- The reader gets one concrete practice or reflection prompt.
- Social variants preserve nuance instead of flattening into hot takes.
- Lacey has reviewed tone, boundaries, and public comfort.

## Release Cadence

Start with one substantial post every two weeks for 12 weeks. Cross-post a
shorter social variant the same week, then one follow-up prompt or carousel the
next week. This creates a steady rhythm without forcing weekly longform writing
before the voice is established.
